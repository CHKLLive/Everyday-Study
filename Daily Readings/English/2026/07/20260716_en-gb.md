# Forest City Network School resident defends community amidst immigration investigation

ISKANDAR PUTERI: American entrepreneur Nourah Smith, 28, moved into Network School in Forest City earlier this year.

Smith said she found more than just a place to stay. She found a global community of entrepreneurs, software developers and artificial intelligence (AI) enthusiasts sharing ideas and building businesses.

She is one of more than 260 foreigners living in the purpose-built community in Forest City.

She said the school's collaborative environment had helped her grow professionally while giving new life to Forest City, which was once criticised for its low occupancy.

Her account comes as Network School faces intense public scrutiny following allegations that some members were Israelis who entered Malaysia using second passports.

Johor Menteri Besar Datuk Onn Hafiz Ghazi has called for a thorough investigation, while the Home Ministry, Immigration Department and police are looking into possible immigration violations.

Despite the controversy, Smith told the New Straits Times that Network School operated legally and should not be judged based on the nationality of a handful of people.

Instead, she said every resident should be assessed based on whether they complied with Malaysian laws.

"Judge the person, not the passport," Smith said, adding that members respected local laws and customs during their stay in Malaysia.

Network School describes itself as a "society as a service" — a residential community that combines accommodation, workspaces and educational programmes under a single membership.

Members pay from US$1,500 a month (about RM6,400) for shared accommodation or US$3,000 a month for private rooms at the Forest City Marina Hotel, which has been converted into the community's main campus.

The package includes accommodation, three meals a day, a 24-hour gym, high-speed internet, co-working spaces, a library, childcare facilities and a packed schedule of lectures, hackathons, coding workshops, language classes and wellness programmes.

The community primarily attracts entrepreneurs, software engineers, start-up founders, cryptocurrency developers and AI researchers from around the world.

Smith said the attraction lay not only in the facilities but also in being surrounded by like-minded people capable of discussing emerging technologies, digital governance, blockchain and AI without lengthy introductions.

Network School has also developed its own internal digital platform to organise events and manage community activities, replacing third-party services it previously relied on.

Smith said this reflected the community's intention to establish a long-term presence rather than operate as a temporary gathering of digital nomads.

"Several businesses in Forest City have also benefited from the influx of residents.

"Restaurants, cafés, convenience stores and transport operators have seen increased patronage since Network School began operating, while accommodation units that had remained vacant are now occupied," she told the NST.

"We have injected fresh economic activity into Forest City and contributed to Johor's ambition of becoming a regional technology and innovation hub," she added.

However, Smith acknowledged that Malaysian authorities must investigate allegations of immigration or other legal breaches.

Forest City developer Country Garden Pacificview Sdn Bhd has also pledged full cooperation with investigators and said it would enforce its legal and contractual rights should any breach of Malaysian laws, immigration rules, licensing conditions or land-use requirements be established.
